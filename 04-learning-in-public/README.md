## Why I'm Doing This

I spent 4+ years in Customer Success — owning renewals, onboarding, health scores, and escalations for B2B SaaS. I watched products get built, saw what confused customers, felt the gap between what was shipped and what was needed.

I started building tools to fill that gap. Then I realised: I'd been doing product work all along. I just didn't have the title.

This series is my attempt to:
1. Synthesise what I've learned by building HospitalityIQ and automating CS workflows
2. Think out loud in a format that forces clarity
3. Connect with other PM-in-transition folks who are doing the same

## The Series Format

Each post follows a **Problem → Insight → Application** structure:
- What problem I encountered (real, specific)
- The framework or thinking that helped me navigate it

Posts blend PM + Customer Success — because I believe the best PMs understand what happens *after* the product ships.

## Selected Posts

### Day 6 — The Moment I Stopped Asking "What Do You Want?"

> *"The question 'what do you want?' is almost always the wrong question in discovery. What you want is to see what they do."*

In my early interviews for HospitalityIQ, I kept asking GMs what features they wanted. I got wishlists. Feature requests. Things that sounded like what they'd seen in other tools.

The shift came when I started asking: "Walk me through your last Monday morning." That's when I heard about the 3-hour manual data pull. Nobody had said "I want an AI briefing." They said "I hate that I have to do this every day before I can think about anything else."

That's the job to be done. The rest is execution.

---

### Day 10 — The CS → PM Translation Layer

> *"CS gave me the data. PM gave me the language to act on it."*

The hardest part of moving from CS to PM isn't learning the frameworks. It's learning to translate what you already know.

When I was in CS: "Customers keep asking why their reports take so long" → I'd escalate it and log it as feedback.

In PM mode: That's a recurring friction point affecting 40% of our accounts, creating a 2-day delay in their weekly planning cycle, which is directly correlated with our 90-day churn risk segment. Here's the opportunity size. Here's the build cost. Here's my recommendation.

Same observation. Completely different frame.
